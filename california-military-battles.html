Military Engagements of California, 1769–1840

Treating Indigenous Combatants as Military Actors

110+ engagements spanning the Spanish and Mexican periods. From the first clash at San Diego (1769) through the Clear Lake Massacre (1843), this map traces military power as it was actually contested — with indigenous intelligence networks, terrain knowledge, alliances, and strategic objectives.

Phase 1 (1769–1790): Coastal resistance. Kumeyaay, Toypurina, Tenoqui.
Phase 2 (1795–1805): Bay Area resistance. Sacalanes and East Bay peoples vs. mission expansion.
Phase 3 (1806–1820): Interior campaigns. Moraga era. Horse economies, multi-village alliances.
Yuma (1781–1820): Colorado River — the greatest Spanish disaster in California.
Maritime: Naval defense, English corsairs, Bouchard's privateer raid.
Mexican Period (1821–1840): Chumash Revolt, Estanislao, Vallejo's Sonoma frontier, late frontier violence.
Sources: C-A manuscripts (Internet Archive); Cook, Colonial Expeditions (1960); Bancroft, History of California, I–V; Beebe & Senkewicz, Lands of Promise and Despair (2001); Pueblo Papers (201 Sal letters); Fages, Colorado River Campaign (Priestley, 1913); Vallejo, Recuerdos Históricos.
