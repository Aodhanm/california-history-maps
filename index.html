California History Maps

Interactive maps of Spanish- and Mexican-era California expeditions and frontier geography, reconstructed from primary archival sources and secondary scholarship

Four interactive maps drawn from an ongoing research project on colonial California (1769–1846). Routes, engagements, and settlements are plotted from expedition diaries, the Archives of California (BANC MSS C–A), and the standard scholarship; most markers carry primary-source detail in their pop-ups. Click any map to explore.

About & Sources

These maps accompany a long-running research project on Spanish- and Mexican-era California — the presidios, missions, and pueblos of the northern frontier of New Spain and, after 1821, of Mexican California. They are built to make the geography of the archival record legible: where expeditions actually went, which Indigenous communities they passed through, and how competing empires pressed on San Francisco Bay.

Method

  • Routes are reconstructed stop-by-stop from expedition diaries and S. F. Cook's route studies, with campsite and village identifications drawn from Cook, Gifford & Schenck (1926), and Kroeber (1925).
  • Engagements and imperial activity are plotted from the Archives of California (BANC MSS C–A) — the Savage transcript copies that are the sole surviving record of colonial California's government after the 1906 destruction of the originals — and from Bancroft.
  • Coordinates are best-available reconstructions, not surveyed points; where a location is uncertain the pop-up says so.
